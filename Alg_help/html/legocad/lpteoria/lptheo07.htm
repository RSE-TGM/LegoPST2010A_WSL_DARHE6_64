Steam turbines and related modules: generalities

 

A steam turbine consists of a series of stages, through which steam is passing, in which the initial pressure is progressively broken down to exhaust pressure, each stage contributing to the power output of the machine.

Each stage consists of a nozzle (stationary blades) and a bucket (rotating blades).

The fractional reaction Rx of a stage is defined as the ratio of the energy released in the bucket passage to the total stage energy.

Turbine stages can be classified as impulse stages (Rx = 0) and reaction stages (Rx > 0).

The overall efficiency of a steam turbine is a combination of the stage efficiency and the reheat factor. In any stage the efficiency depends on the physical dimensions and the thermodynamic conditions. The efficiency of a stage is equal to the nozzle-bucket efficiency minus the loss due to several other items: the rotation loss (windage) of the stage, the diaphragm-packing leakage loss caused by part of the steam passing through the diaphragm packing (interstage gland), the moisture loss, the nozzle-end loss in a partial-admission stage and the exhaust loss.

In an actual nozzle-bucket system many deviations from the ideal case occur. For example it is impossible for the entrance angle of the steam to the bucket to be in the plane of the wheel, because there would be mechanical interference between the bucket and the nozzle, and it is impossible for the steam to leave the bucket at an angle of 0° with respect to the plane of rotation of the bucket, because if it did, there would be no opportunity for the flow of steam to progress axially through the turbine. Several other deviations from the ideal case exist. Therefore the efficiency will not be 1 even at optimum velocity ratio.

Steam turbines and related modules: impulse stage

 

In an impulse stage of a steam turbine the stationary blades convert potential energy of the steam to kinetic energy by reducing the flow area, which increases the velocity. In addition to accelerating the steam, these blades direct the flow into the rotating blades at the proper angle. The rotating blades then convert the kinetic energy to torque. All of the stage pressure drop, thus all of the stage energy is on the stationary blades. There is no pressure drop across the rotating blades and no change in relative steam velocity.

The following assumptions are made:

superheated steam behaves as a perfect gas

no mass storage

no inertia

no energy storage in the fluid

no heat transfer between fluid and mechanical parts

The control valve can be activated in a sequential mode so the turbine can be operated with both active and inactive arcs of admission (partial arc admission), or the steam can be admitted to the entire periphery of the first stage as the control valves are opened and the arc of admission does not vary (full arc admission).

Of course the total control valves mass flow rate must be equal to the impulse stage mass flow rate.

superheated steam behaves as a perfect gas

no mass storage

no inertia

no energy storage in the fluid
