NUMERICAL METHODS

The mathematical models developed to characterise various physical processes such as transient fluid flow, heat conduction and component models are comprised of a coupled set of partial differential equations, ordinary differential equations and algebraical equations.

An analytical solution does not exist for this equation set and the use of a numerical solution method is required.

The main topics of numerical solution methods and attendant assumption are shortly presented.

The differential equations must be replaced by "finite difference" equations with respect to the time and to the space, which are algebraical equations.

The algebraical equations may be obtained by two different routes:

*

the first method uses finite difference representations of the partial differential equations (distributed parameters equations);

*

the second method derives the equations by integrating the differential equation terms on a finite number of control volumes, called cells (lumped parameters equations).

 The resultant equations can be identical in both cases if conservation-law-form differential equations are used with the first method.

The independent variables, namely "time" and "space", can assume only a discrete and finite sequence of values, that is to say a calculation grid must be set.

The dependent variables will be computed only for these points and for these instants, which can be equally spaced (constant time step) or unequally spaced (variable time step).

When the first method is used, the finite difference approximation of the space derivatives (backward, forward or central difference) must be chosen.

When the second method is used, a "staggered mesh" computation grid for the control volumes is often adopted.

Two finite difference approximation for the time derivatives (backward or forward difference) are possible.

With the forward difference approximation the solution method is explicit, that is to say the solution at every point of the given time depends on the values of the solution at several points of the preceding time step.

TWO PHASE - FLOW MODELS

The following descriptions of two-phase flow are widely used:

* thermodynamic equilibrium models (the phases are always in thermodynamic equilibrium conditions), then:

* liquid is always saturated,

* vapour is always saturated.

* non-equilibrium models (the phases can be, during the transients, in non-equilibrium conditions), then:

* liquid can be saturated, non saturated or with bubbles;

* vapour can be saturated or superheated.

 

Various two-phase flow models can be distinguished:

* the two-fluid models

* models based on imposed velocity profiles, depending on the choice of kinematic properties; there are:

* no local relative velocity (homogeneous model, Bankoff model),

* correlation for the relative velocity given by means of a slip ratio vg/vl (as a function of pressure and equilibrium quality), a weighted drift velocity Ugj and a distribution coefficient C0 (as a function of pressure and of the physical properties of the fluid, drift-flux or Zuber and Findlay model), and a void fraction (as a function of pressure and equilibrium quality, Martinelli and Nelson).

 

For applications in the industrial processes the more used two-phase models are:

* homogeneous equilibrium model (HEM)

* thermal equilibrium slip or Drift flux (natural circulation and low pressure)

* thermal non-equilibrium models with interaction between the phases given by empirical correlations.

 

WATER-STEAM AND OTHER FLUIDS PROPERTIES

The thermodynamic state of a fluid is completely determined by two independent intensive properties (for instance specific enthalpy and pressure). The dependent properties can be calculated by means of tables or by an analytical formulation.

It is worthwhile to note that the various thermodynamic properties are not independent of each other. For example, when the pressure p and the temperature T are chosen as the independent variables of the formulation, then expressions, called derived function, for the specific volume, entropy, enthalpy and all other thermodynamic properties may be derived directly by partial differentiation of the so-called canonical function g = g(p;T), where g is the specific free enthalpy (Gibbs function). If the analytical formulation is presented in terms of these canonical functions, the thermodynamic consistency is maintained.

The most useful properties of a fluid are:

* density (r)

* specific enthalpy (h)

* specific entropy (s)

* temperature (T)

* viscosity (m)

* thermal conductivity (l)

* specific heat at constant pressure (Cp)

* the partial derivative of density with respect to pressure (at constant entropy)

* the partial derivative of density with respect to entropy (at constant pressure)
