MODULE STAZ

CARD A

Model Generalities

IMPULSE STAGE OF STEAM TURBINE

 

The STAZ module simulates the behaviour of an impulse stage of steam turbine; it belongs to the thermodynamic standard library.

As impulse stage is intended a stage in which the conversion of enthalpy possessed by steam into kinetic energy exclusively occurs in guide blading (also called stator or nozzle arch). The kinetic energy acquired by steam is transferred to bucket blading (rotor). Usually only the first stage of an high pressure turbine consists of one impulse stage. On the contrary, both in following stages of high pressure turbine and those having mean and low pressure generally are reaction stages. In the last one a further conversion of enthalpy energy on bucket blading is obtained.

By using a merely thermodynamic statement, the STAZ module simulates the functioning of the steam turbine impulse stage. In particular it simulates the first stage of an high pressure turbine known as control stage. In fact in the STAZ module is included whole inlet steam system into the turbine (inlet valves, piping feeding the sector in which nozzle arch is subdivided and related regulating valves).

From a structural point of view, steam inlet system into the turbine consists of one or more inlet pipes which let in steam into different feeding pipes equipped with one's regulating valve. Each feeding pipe is connected with a particular sector of nozzle arch (see figure 1).

figure 1

The model is based on the following main hypotheses:

- steam behaviour is assimilate to a perfect gas one

- absence of accumulation phenomenon (mass, momentum, energy) in the system

- absence of thermal exchange between steam and metallic surface of connection pipes

- isenthalpic expansion of steam up to inlet into the nozzles

- head losses along connection piping are supposed to be concentrated in regulating valves and consequently at their outlet the pressure is coincident with pressure at the nozzle inlet.

A further detailed discussion about validity of the above mentioned hypotheses is available in "Turbine Mathematical Model in Temperature Calculation of Steam Inside the Impulse Rotor Chamber" by R.Terrazzani, A.Beato and M.Colombini published by ENEL Milano October 1979. A synthesis is carried out in the module theoretical documentation.

Steam turbines usually have two inlet pipes. The STAZ module allows to operate with two inlet valves to connect with two steam inlet piping. So, it is possible to reproduce the actual configuration or use only one inlet valve connected with only one equivalent inlet piping. This configuration can be used when are not interesting the problems concerning the feeding dissymmetry.

For every feeding piping in parallel configuration the flow rate is calculated as regards to:

- opening and geometry of regulating valve associated to the piping

- nozzles geometry

- enthalpy and pressure upstream the inlet valve

- pressure at nozzles outlet, so in rotor chamber.

It is useful to note that the model takes into account the possibility in which valves and/or nozzles are under critical functioning conditions (sonic conditions).

As already mentioned, in determining the state evolution of steam along every feeding piping, the expansion into the regulating valve is considered isenthalpic. On the contrary, enthalpy at the nozzle outlet is calculated beginning from the inlet one (valve outlet), by using the thermodynamic efficiency definition of steam transformation which is considered almost isenthropic. In this way it is possible to quantify the efficiency as quadratic function of ratio between the revolving speed of a turbine blade median point and the absolute speed of steam at the nozzles outlet. The enthalpy in rotor chambers is then calculated mixing the enthalpies of different pipes.

Once calculated the quantities interesting every single feeding piping, the quantities characterizing the stage total behaviour are calculated too.

On the basis of assigned manufacturing and functioning characteristics as well as some boundary thermodynamic and physical conditions (like pressure and enthalpy of supplied steam and its pressure inside the rotor) the following are calculated:

- values of flow rate flowing into the stage

- enthalpy value inside the rotor chamber

- mechanic power generated in the stage.

Two functioning conditions are possible:

- start-up: when turbine revolving speed is variable and the turbine is uncoupled from alternator (see note 1)

- running: when turbine revolving speed is constant and the turbine is coupled with alternator.

Two different conditions of turbine feeding are foreseen:

- steam inlet on whole nozzle ring synchronizing the opening of regulating valves (functioning in total arc)

- steam inlet in a gradual manner on different nozzle ring sectors, by opening regulating valves with a suitably sequence (functioning in partial arc).

During start-up and under low loads (when considerable thermal shocks on the turbine could be caused by a sequence feeding), the total arc functioning is used.

Under transient condition the module also allows the changeover from total to partial arc functioning and vice versa.

The first five residual equations are related to overall quantities characterizing the whole stage. The utilized equations are:

1) calculation equation of total flow rate flowing into the stage

2) calculation equation of enthalpy in the rotor chamber

3) calculation equation of power generated inside the stage

4) calculation equation of flow rate flowing through the nozzle arc sectors fed by the first feeding piping

5) calculation equation of flow rate flowing through the nozzle arc sectors fed by the (possible) second feeding piping.

It is useful to precise the meanings of WAM1 and WAM2 flow rates calculated through the equations 4 and 5. Each of these flow rates (in case of two inlet piping) represents the total flow rate flowing through these nozzle arc sectors fed by the same inlet piping. In particular an half nozzle arc sector is assigned to each inlet piping. Even if only one inlet piping is considered, the calculation of WAM1 and WAM2 flow rate keeps separate. In this case each of them has merely the meaning of the flow rate flowing through half nozzle arc.

Among own output variables, the module also has the pressure upstream nozzles and the position of regulating valves. To each of these output variables does not correspond an equation solved by LEGO code simultaneously with rest part of the system. Therefore in coupling these output variables with input ones, coming from other modules, some precaution are required.

To simulate the whole turbine, besides the STAZ module, also the TREN module simulating reaction stages has to be used. Bleedings in a reaction stage are simulated by a suitable use of VALV or SPIL modules (see related documents).

When the simulation is referred to a start-up condition the MART module has to be utilized too. This module evaluates the (possible) turbine speed variation (during transient condition) taking into account the turbine inertia and its generated mechanics power. In start-up simulation, use of a model representing the turbine speed regulation is also required (see note 1 for a description of start-up conditions).

A monitoring of metal temperature both for casing and rotor can be done by using the nozzles upstream pressure and utilizing the FLEX, TSML and MEST modules.

The FLEX module determines the temperature and thermal exchange coefficient of steam by using pressure and enthalpy values upstream the regulating valves of flow rate flowing into the stage. Afterwards, the TSML and MEST modules calculate the thermal range on casing and rotor and, for the last one, is also calculated the wear consequent to a fixed transient.

The STAZ module includes the data needing in simulating of control stages related to the following turbines:

- TOSI (320 and 660 MW)

- ANSALDO (320 MW).

If different turbine control stages has to be simulate, user must built a subprogram including data concerning the quantities listed in section on physical and geometrical data, in accordance with "User subprograms" section. In building such subroutine user can imitate one of those related to the three control stages carried out in "Data acquisition subroutines" section.

Note 1

Progressive and controlled heating of turbine different parts is the basic problem during start-up operations.

Time required in starting the machine, to reach full load conditions, is determined by needs to obtain its progressive heating. This means that a progressive inlet of steam into the turbine has to be carried out both in thermal stabilization periods (with revolving speed lower than target one), and in slow increasing load after paralleling.

During start-up operations the following conditions are met: at the beginning inlet and regulating valves must be closed.

The turbine is driven by a barring device with an angular speed equal to few runs per minute.

In machine resetting is automatically arranged the speed control by means of inlet valves while regulating valves are fully opened. When the fixed speed is set up by operator, speed default (difference between pre-set and actual speed), suitably increased, controls the inlet valves opening. To higher and higher set up speeds corresponds a progressive opening of inlet valves as well as increased steam flow rate into the turbine.

When turbine speed is close to 2700 rpm (nominal speed), the operator actuates the speed regulation changeover from inlet valves to regulating ones.

Steam flow rate which allows to keep constant the turbine speed (2700 rpm) is the WAVV flow rate mentioned in physical and geometrical data section. The corresponding inlet valve lift is the ZETAM quantity mentioned in same section.

Inlet valve fully open while, at the same time, regulating valves begin to close so that a speed regulation is assured.

Speed regulation changeover from inlet valves to regulating ones, is carried out through a suitable control of electrohydraulic regulation system.

Afterwards, when target speed is reached, the parallel coupling with network is carried out. Start-up operations can be considered settled.

MODULE STAZ

CARD C

Physical and Geometrical Data

In addition to initial values of input and output variables, user must assign the following data:

[ ] TIP_TURB string of characters for selection of simulating control stage type:

- ANSALDO320 to simulate the control stage of 320 MW Ansaldo turbine

- TOSI320 to simulate the control stage of a 320 MW Tosi turbine

- TOSI660 to simulate the control stage of a 660 MW Tosi turbine

A further string can be assigned if user has to simulate a different type of turbine control stage.

The quantities ZETAM, PAVV, HAVV and WAVV thereinafter mentioned characterize the behaviour of turbine inlet valves. On this subject it is useful to precise how inlet valve can be described as consisting of two valves. The smaller one (pilot valve) is used for turbine start-up whereas the bigger one is the real on-off valve. In the first lift step (values from zero to ZETAM) the valve discharge coefficient is the pilot valve one and for higher values it is the on-off valve one.

The quantities ZETAM, PAVV and HAVV defines the inlet valve dimensioning in agreement with the fully opening of pilot valve that is for a ZETAM opening value by using the following relation:

CVA(ZETAM) = WAVV/SQRT(1-R)/SQRT(PAVV*ROAV)

where:

- R indicates the so called critical ratio (a constant in the adopted model)

- ROAV is the density value corresponding to HAVV and PAVV

- CVA is the valve discharge capacity (to avoid possible misunderstanding it is perhaps useful to emphasize that CVA has the meaning of the specific discharge capacity per the valve cross section area).

[p.u.] ZETAM lift value of inlet valve throwing with start-up flow rate WAVV is flowing (pilot valve fully opened)
[Pa] PAVV inlet steam pressure
[J/kg] HAVV inlet steam enthalpy
[kg/s] WAVV steam flow rate given by only one inlet valve with ZETAM lift, PAVV pressure upstream inlet valve and HAVV steam enthalpy.

When lift values are lower than ZETAM, the valve outlet coefficient is calculated with the following linear relation:

CVA(ZETA) = CVA(ZETAM)*ZETA/ZETAM

when lift values are higher than ZETAM it is used a parabolic curve connecting the CVA value related to ZETAM with the CVA value related to a situation which foreseen an inlet valve fully opened (the CVA value related to a fully open valve condition is calculated inside the module by using the data related to the nominal conditions DPVE, PSHNOM, TSHNOM and WNOM thereinafter described in physical and geometrical data section).

These quantities assume control only in start-up simulation (see note 1).

All turbine data needing for simulation (describing the manufacturing characteristics of different impulse stages implemented in the module) are included in some subroutines of STAZ module (DXANS320 subroutine for impulse stage of a 320 MW Ansaldo turbine, DXTOS320 for a 320 MW Tosi turbine and DXTTOS660 for a 660 MW Tosi turbine).

The listing of these subroutines is thereinafter mentioned in "Data acquisition subroutines" section.

If a control stage for a different turbine has to be simulate, user must write a subprogram including the same type of informations of existing subroutines, referring to the interested control stage.

MODULE STAZ

CARD F 

Residuals

From a normal point of view, residual the number of present residuals is not fixed up but it varies at varying of number of regulating valves. Nevertheless, the first five residuals are fixed up, independent from number of regulating valves and constitute the actual module equations.

The first residual has the following form:

RNI(1) = (WAMM-WAM1-WAM2)

(normalization factor = W0)

The residual RNI(2) has the following form:

RNI(2) = (HCRT - HMISC)

(normalization factor = H0/100)

where HMISC has the meaning of the enthalpy obtained by mixing enthalpies of flow rate flowing on the different nozzles arc sectors.

The residual RNI(3) has two different expressions as a function of the considered number of inlet piping. So, in case of only one inlet piping:

RNI(3) = QMEC-WAMM*(HAMM-HCRT)

(normalization factor = W0*H0)

while in case of two inlet piping:

RNI(3) = QMEC-(WAM1*(HAM1-HCRT)+WAM2*(HAM2-HCRT))

(normalization factor = W0*H0)

The residual RNI(4) can be formally written as:

- only one inlet piping

RNI(4) = WAM1 - f4(PAMM, HAMM, ALZS, FARC, PCRT)

(normalization factor = W0)

- two inlet piping

RNI(4) = WAM1 - f4(PAM1, HAM1, ALZS, FARC, PCRT)

(normalization factor = W0)

Similarly, the RNI(5) residual can be formally written as:

- only one inlet piping

RNI(5) = WAM2 - f5(PAMM, HAMM, ALZS, FARC, PCRT)

(normalization factor = W0)

- two inlet pipings

RNI(5) = WAM2 - f5(PAM2, HAM2, ALZS, FARC, PCRT);

(normalization factor = W0)

Remaining residuals equations can be used for monitoring the values of the i-th regulating valve lift and upstream pressure of arc sector supplied by the i-th regulating valve. So the NVALV equation for lifts and NVALV equations for the pressures are present.

RNI(5+i) = AR_i - f(ALZS, FARC) i = 1...NVALV

RNI(5+NVALV+i) = PU_i - f(PAM, HAM, PCRT, ALZS, FARC) i = 1...NVALV
