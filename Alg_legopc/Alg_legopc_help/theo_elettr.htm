Module assumptions

With the exception of purely resistive loads, all other loads, after their energisation, exhibit a transient absorption of active and reactive power whose amplitude may reach a value of several times the steady-state one.

The transient is, in general, of short duration (1-2 s). However, especially when air conditioning and refrigerator apparatuses are energised, it can last a longer time, hindering the regular restoration of the network.

Induction motor

The main hypotheses used in this module are:

magnetic saturation effects are negligible

magnetic hysteresis and eddy currents are negligible

the stator windings are sinusoidally distributed as far as the mutual effects with the rotor are concerned

the rotor has a symmetrical structure.

These assumptions are generally accepted in stability studies.

The currents induced in the shorted rotor windings produce a field with the same number of poles as that produced by the stator winding. Rotor windings may therefore be modelled by an equivalent three-phase winding.

Network

Module assumptions

The system is assumed to be balanced; this allows a single phase representation.

The electromagnetic transients associated with the transmission network elements (lines and transformers) decay very rapidly in comparison with electromechanical transients due to machines oscillations. Indeed, network transients will have died out by the time the solution of the swing equation is advanced by one time step. Therefore, it is usually adequate to regard the network, during the electromechanical transients conditions, as though it were passing directly from one steady state to another. The fundamental frequency variations may be regarded as micro-processes, and only the variation in the envelopes (amplitude modulation) of currents and voltage waveforms are considered for stability analysis.

Therefore, the network representation is similar to that for power-flow analysis and the most convenient form of network representation is in terms of the node admittance matrix Y. The network equation is

The elements of the matrix Y are constant except for changes introduced by network switching and transformer tap changing operations.

The admittance matrix is sparse with the degree of sparsity increasing with the network size, it is singular if floating (i.e., if there are no shunt branches to ground), it has weak diagonal dominance, and it is symmetrical if there are no phase-shifting transformers.

Any phase shift due to transformer connection is not represented. In radial networks, such phase shifts do not affect power-flow analysis since currents and voltages are shifted by the same angle. In close-loop networks, utilities take special care to connect transformer windings so that there is no net phase shift introduced in a common direction round a loop; otherwise, circulating current will flow, which is normally unacceptable.

 

Simulation of faults

The negative- and zero-sequence voltages and currents throughout the system are generally not of interest in stability studies. Therefore, it is unnecessary to simulate the complete negative- and zero-sequence networks in system stability simulations. Their effects may be represented by equivalent impedances (Z2 and Z0) as viewed at the fault point F. these impedances are combined appropriately depending on the type of fault and inserted as the effective fault impedance Zef in the positive-sequence network as shown in Fig. 1.

Fig. 1. Representation of faults in system stability studies

 

A fault at or near a bus is thus simulated by appropriately changing the self-admittance of the bus. This representation gives the correct voltages and currents in the positive-sequence network. Since there are no negative- or zero-sequence voltages generated in the system and the negative- and zero-sequence currents that flow during unbalanced faults are driven by the positive -sequence voltage sources, the positive-sequence power outputs computed represent the actual power supplied electrically by the machines and include all negative and zero-sequence resistance losses supplied electrically.
