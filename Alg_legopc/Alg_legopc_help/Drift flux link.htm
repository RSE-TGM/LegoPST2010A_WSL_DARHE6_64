Two-phase flow: drift flux model

 

This is the classical formulation of Zuber and Findlay for the representation of a non-homogeneous two-phase flow.

This representation gives sufficient accuracy for moderate transients in which the mixture evolves in a quasi-steady-state fashion and even during fast transients it has not been shown to lead to great inaccuracies.

As well known, the drift between the phases causes energy and momentum to cross the mixture centre-of-mass plane and generates in the corresponding conservation equations additional terms depending both on the average void fraction a and the so-called "total drift velocity" Ugj.

Therefore a relationship between the void fraction and the drift velocity is necessary as well as the determination of the last one by means of adequate correlations. In the basic drift-flux approach a linkage is established between the average void fraction of the mixture and the two typical average parameters accounting for the local drift (Vgj) and the non-uniformity of the velocity and void fraction profiles across the duct cross-section (C0):

One remarks that the "total drift" Ugj includes contributions due to both the average local drift Vgj and the velocity profile (the (C0 - 1)J term).

It is evident that a proper evaluation of the two drift parameters C0 and Vgj becomes essential to obtain a realistic determination of the void fraction (i.e. of the density) of the two-phase flow.

To this aim several correlations are available, generally developed for specific applications and then strongly dependent on the considered geometrical and thermal-hydraulic conditions. But if the "drift-flux" model must be used in the simulation of the whole working situations of a plant system, considerable difficulties from the computational view-point could arise utilising different specifically flow-regime dependent correlations. To avoid this problem two empirical flow-regime independent "drift- flux" correlations have been considered and implemented in LEGOCAD models:

* the Zuber-Dix correlation

* the Chexall-Lellouche correlation

covering the full range of pressures, flows and void fractions and qualified against several sets of steady-state test data coming from a wide range of initial conditions and geometries.
