Furnace thermal balance model:

Modules referred to: CCCM

The goal is the evaluation of radiation heat transfer as well as temperature and radiating species concentration distributions in the furnace. The furnace walls temperatures, the inlet and outlet flow rates are boundary conditions.

The models for analysing heat transfer in industrial furnaces are of three types:

* the "stirred vessel" (zero-dimensional) model, which yields only the total heat transfer rate without providing information on the local heat flux distribution;

* the "plug-flow" (one-dimensional) model, which is capable of predicting the local heat flux in the furnace along the flow direction;

* the multidimensional model which can predict two-dimensional heat flux distribution at the load surface.

The first two models are being used routinely in engineering design calculations, and the one-dimensional model has been adopted as standard model in the LEGOCAD modules library and will be discussed in greater detail.

Various kinds of fuel are considered, such as natural gas, fuel oil and coal.

The burners tilting is taken into account.

The furnace is divided in control volumes, called cells, along the main gas path.

 

The simulated phenomena are:

* fluid mass conservation

* fluid energy conservation

* radiative and convective heat transfer

* combustion chemical reactions

 

The main hypotheses are:

* no mass storage

* no heat storage

The evaluation of the unburnt fuel Gubf and air Guba mass flow rates is based on the stoichiometric air-fuel ratio. When the actual ratio of the total entering air and the total entering fuel is greater than the stoichiometric ratio, the fuel entirely burns and an excess air flow rate Guba leaves the cell; when the actual ratio is less than the stoichiometric one, an unburnt fuel flow rate Gubf exits the cell. If the furnace is fed with a mixture of fuels, the assumption is that as first natural gas burns, as second fuel oil and finally coal.

burners tilt effect

The burners tilt shifts the flame upwards or downwards in the furnace. The effect rises when the fuel flow rate increases, because the flame length grows. The tilt effect is taken into account by partly shifting the inlet fuel flow rate upwards or downwards in proportion to the tilt angle.

 

 

coal combustion

The coal combustion has three main phases:

* volatile matter release

* volatile matter combustion

* solid particles combustion

 

The volatile matter release and combustion is nearly instantaneous, whereas the solid particles combustion takes some time, that is to say solid particles do not completely burn in the same cell where they are injected.

Radiation heat transfer is due to gaseous contributions, mainly due to CO2 and H2O, and particulate contributions, which usually result from coal (char), soot and fly-ash. Radiation from fly-ash particles exceeds substantially the contribution of both triatomic combustion gases, as well as char and soot particles.
