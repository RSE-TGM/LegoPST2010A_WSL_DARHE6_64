MODULE PLEN

CARD A

Model Generalities

GAS DUCT VOLUME

 

The PLEN module simulates the big cavities in which a gaseous fluid mixing occurs.

In these components the mass accumulation phenomenon is prevailing.

The model is based on the following main hypotheses:

- the surface of cavities is adiabatic

- perfectly mixed gas in the volume so that it is possible to consider its temperature unvarying

- for the fluid in the cavity it is valid the perfect gas law.

The utilized equations are:

1) the equation of gas mass conservation

2) the equation of gas energy conservation.

In applying the equation 1, it is presumed gas temperature varies so slowly that density variations are not influenced by it.

In applying the equation 2, instead, enthalpy variations coming from pressure variations are not considered.

Such hypotheses are valid if the module application is limited to air chambers or precipitators, where there are not sudden temperature variations and pressure variations are so low that fluid enthalpy is not influenced by.

Therefore the module is not suitable to simulate a combustion furnace or a second gas pass zone of a boiler, where the temperature variations are quick and considerable. Instead the model keeps its validity in case of back-flow in the ducts at the cavity inlet.

INPUT-OUTPUT SCHEME

 

NWIN = number of ducts coming in

NWOU = number of ducts coming out

 

 

PHYSICAL MEANING OF MODULE INPUT-OUTPUT VARIABLES

[kg/s] WE(i) (i = 1, NWIN) air-gas flow rate in the i-th duct coming into the plenum
[kg/s] WU(j) (j = 1, NWOU) air-gas flow rate in the j-th duct coming out of the plenum
[K] TE(i) (i = 1, NWIN) air-gas temperature in the i-th duct coming into the plenum
[Pa] PFPL fluid pressure in the plenum
[K] TFPL fluid temperature in the plenum

Note

The perfect gas law is expressed by the following state equation:

P*V = n*R*T

where P is the gas pressure, V its volume, T its temperature, R is the gas universal constant and n is the number of considered moles of gas.

Dividing for the gas mass M we have:

P/RO = (n*R/M)*T

where RO is the gas density.

Assuming:

where M/n is the gas molecular mass, we have:

P/RO = RGAS*T
