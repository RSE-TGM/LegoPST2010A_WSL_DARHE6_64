Other fluids properties

A great deal of fluids must be considered, such as air, oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, methane and other hydrocarbons, fuel oil, combustion products, etc...

Density determination

For real gases and vapours the perfect gas law:

pV = RT

is often used.

This equation, which applies to both pure components and mixtures, is reasonably accurate, when the temperature is clearly higher than the critical temperature, up to a pressure of about 2 bar for most compounds and even higher for small, nonassociating molecules.

Leaving this zone this equation is no longer valid and other state equations with two parameters, such as the Van Der Walls equation and the often used Redlich and Kwong equation

have been proposed.

The equation with two parameters have the advantage that the parameters can be expressed in terms of the critical coordinates pcrit, Tcrit and Vcrit imposing that for the critical point two conditions are fulfilled:

which determine a1 and a2 as a function of pcrit, Tcrit and Vcrit.

These equations moreover can be expressed in terms of reduced variables

It results that if two reduced variables are known, the third one is defined whatever the fluid may be.

From these statements the law of corresponding states arises.

If there were equations of state with two parameters representing the behaviour of real fluids, it would be possible, even without knowing this law, to predict the properties of any fluid based on those of a known fluid, on condition that the critical state be known. Such an universal law does not exist, but there are equations of state which are somehow valid for groups of equivalent bodies, for instance series of hydrocarbons.

Another method of approach is the so-called virial method; it is based on the fact that at a given temperature the product pV tends toward RT when p tends toward zero. Therefore

The virial coefficients a2, a3, etc. are functions of T only.

For liquids empirical correlations are used.

Specific heat capacity determination

For gases the ideal gas heat capacity is computed, which is a function of temperature but not of pressure. The most common analytical equation relating the heat capacity to the temperature is a simple polynomial.

For liquids empirical correlations are used.

Other properties determination

All the other properties are estimated by empirical correlations.
