MODULE PALL

CARD A

Model Generalities

BALL MILL FOR COAL

 

The PALL module simulates the operation of a ball mill to grind the coal destined to combustion in thermal power plants.

For the definition of the mathematical model reference was made to this configuration of the mill: the raw coal is conveyed by a belt at variable speed, it enters the center of the scoop and by centrifugal force it is pushed toward the area where balls (or rollers) grind it and reaches the external edge of the scoop and the slot between the scoop and the mill case. Hot air (primary air) outflows from this opening, rejecting large size coal towards the grinding area and transporting the lower size coal towards the classifier. Coal particles in the classifier, have radial spreading according to the size: larger ones, having higher centrifugal force, are at the outside and fall in the scoop, smaller ones are placed inward and are transported by air along coal dust ducts.

In the mathematical model adoped, the following hypothesis are assumed:

i - The coal dust in the grinding area has a size distribution where each particle can be identified by a linear dimension (D) called particle diameter;

ii - The size distribution is uniform in the area where grinding takes place (zero-dimensional model); therefore we can assume to have a unique diametral density function N, depending on the diameter of particles D and on time t, where N(D,t) * deltaD represents the mass of coal particles in the grinding area of diameters within D and D + deltaD.

iii - The coal dust flow rate from the central area of the scoop to the grinding area is function of the centrifugal force acting on the difference of level between coal masses in the scoop and in the grinding area.

iv - The coal dust mass accumulation in the scoop is considered.

v - Grinding phenomena of coal in the grinding area are treated as a continuous process with a Um grinding speed expressed by the relation Um = dD / dt.

vi - The coal particle mass accumulation in the grinding area is also considered.

vii - The coal rate per diameter unit from the grinding area towards the flue depends on the centrifugal force acting on the level difference between the mass of particles in the grinding area and the mass of particles in the flue.

viii - Primary air through the flue rejects the particles having larger size towards the grinding area (mixing particles according to the zero-dimensional hypothesis) and transports only smaller particles to the classifier, for which the conveyance force by air overcomes the gravity force.

ix - It is considered a mass accumulation in the classifier area. In this phenomenon, different staying times are considered with respect to the distribution of particles sizes.

x - Due to considerable delays in the grinding phenomenon. small delays associated to recyling rates through the flue and the classifier towards the grinding area are neglected.

xi - The classifier selects, with 100% effectiveness, the particle at a break Db diameter depending on the position of the classifier gates.

 

In these hypothesis, the material accumulation in the scoop area and in the classifier area has not been considered (hypothesis iv and ix), since numeric coefficients present in the equations governing the two phenomena are not available.

The equations on which the module is based are the mass conservation equation in the scoop, grinding and flue areas.

 

NOTES ON OPERATION OF THE PALL MODULE

The coal diametral density inside the scoop N(D,t) is recorded by the module in appropriate elements of the DATA vector, which position is calculated on the basis of the number of divisions required by NDIV.

The integral of N versus the diameter represents the coal mass therein contained, whose value is used for the cauculation of the grinding speed Um, required in the evaluation of vector N. Unfortunately, at the first iteration of the steady state, the DATA vector is null, and therefore also the estimated coal mass is null: the content of vector N so calculated is wrong and does not correspond to a steady state condition (and in fact the results of a derivative are physically not plausible).

This problem has been solved forcing the module to make several calculation cycles of vector N until the mass calculated as integral of vector N does not correspond (unless setting of an appropriate threshold, set at 1.E-3 kg) to the one previously used to estimate the vector itself.

The procedure, usually requiring less than ten cycles, is very quick, particularly when the selected number of cells is low and results obtained actually represent a steady state of the system.

This iteration cycle could be performed also in a transient state (making comments of an "IF (KREGIM) THEN" instruction and the corresponding "ENDIF"). Results obtained in transient state with this option are obviously more accurate, but with an extension of calculation times of about 10%, results are subject to neglectable variations (lower than 0.5%).

Physical and Geometrical Data

 

In addition to the initial values of variables, the following data set must be provided:

Coefficients for friction angle calculation

[ ] FI constant term in friction angle equation (default: 0.0344)
[s/kg] FPI angular coefficient in friction angle equation (default: 0.002)

 

Friction-Factor (F ) = FI + FPI *Wcoal 

Hints for initialization: the relationship between pulverized mass flow rate and friction angle is an experimental law. It can be provided by the constructor of the mill or found pointwise by examining the mill working point at several charges and interpolating a line through them. If only the nominal working point is available, only one point in the characteristic is known, calculated as:

F = P / (F Rm w )

where P is the grinding power (by ignoring the mechanical efficiency rate, it’s the nominal motor power at the turning shaft), F is the nominal pressure force of the grinding device, Rm is the grinding zone radius and w is the shaft nominal angular speed. In order to allow a complete definition of the linear characteristic, one should also know the slope of the line (FPI). Nevertheless, as the contribution of this slope is usually not significant, it could, as a first approximation, be kept equal to the default value, improving data refinement only if a particular tuning is necessary for non-nominal work points.

 

Coefficients for breakage law calculation

[ ] NLAW exponent appearing in the relationship expressing the grinding law (default: 2.0)
[ mNLAW+1/s2] CLAW coefficient of proportionality between mass grinding energy and diameter decrease (default: 3.15)

Um = - m DNLAW

m = CLAW -1 (F Rm w F ) / M

where Um is the coal particles breaking speed and M is the total coal mass inside the mill

Hints for initialization: as far as NLAW is concerned, it depends from the comminution theory. The default value corresponds to the Rittiger’s theory which is the most usual. As regards CLAW it could be tuned starting from the default value (valid for ball mills)

 

Geometrical factors

[m³] VT mill volume
[ ] FS1, FS2, FS3 shape adimensional parameters
(defaults: 0.00317476, 0.21434 e 0.247221). They could be considered the same for whole classes of geometrically similar mills

Hints for initialization: VT is only a adimensionalization factor for the shape parameter FS3 and, therefore, isn’t but of lesser importance. FS1, FS2, FS3 are contained in the expression of the efflux coefficient n 0 which has the dimension of [s-1] and ties the spectral mass (N) accumulated inside the grinding zone with the spectral mass flow rate of coal particles through the mill (Wu’). So, the parameter n 0-1 defines the main time constant inside the coal mill: the greater is n 0-1, the slower the dynamics of mass transport (there is another time constant related to mass accumulation in the classifier, but the implied dynamics is far less important).

Modifying shape factors produces the following effects on dynamics:

increasing FS1 accelerates coal transport dynamics
increasing FS2 slows down coal transport dynamics
increasing FS3 has "a priori" an incertain influence on dynamics, but experimental essays show slight effects in the same direction as an increase of FS1.

In conclusion, FS1, FS2 and FS3 can be very useful for tuning the dynamics of a coal mill on the base of experimental trends: while the usage of the theoretical expressions of FS1, FS2, FS3 and n 0-1 is not advised because of various correction coefficients that should be considereded to take into account the many differences between theoretical model and actual coal mill, the abovementioned considerations on the dynamic response could help performing a fine tuning of shape factors starting from the default values provided by the module PALL.

MODULE PALL

CARD F 

Residuals

 

The equations forming the module mathematical model, given in card A1, provide the following residuals:

        RNI(1) = DMASDT / 100. (norm. fact. = 100.)

        RNI(2) = (WUCL - WUCLC) / 10. (norm. fact. = 10.)

Where: DMASDT is the derivative in time of the coal mass contained in the classifier, WUCL is the coal dust rate coming out from the classified (and therefore from the mill) and WUCLC is the value of this flow rate calculated inside the module.
